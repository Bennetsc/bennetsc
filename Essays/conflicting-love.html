Conflicting Love

July 2026

Here's a thought experiment.

To set the stage: two friends, Bob and Fred. Bob falls in love with the person Fred was once in a relationship with (call her Olympia). Worse yet, Olympia has fallen in love with Bob.

Now, from Bob’s perspective:

You just fell in love with Olympia—the woman your friend used to love. Hell, he might still be in love with her. What do you do?

Fred is hurt about this situation—understandably so. He does not want you to have anything to do with Olympia. It hurts him, and he does not want to speak to you because of it. The only thing that would calm him down or regain his trust is if you never talk to Olympia again.

So what do you do? The easy answer is nothing: you respect Fred’s wishes. But let me frame this in a way that muddies the waters.

Forget about Fred for a second. Imagine Fred vanished off the face of the Earth right when you met Olympia. You start dating her, skip forward a few years, and now you’re a family with children.

Suddenly Fred reappears. He tells you he does not approve of the relationship because he used to be with Olympia. Then he pulls out a magic rewind button that would take time back to right before you met her, so you could redo life without her—but with Fred still in it.

He gives you two choices:

Press the button, go back to how things were, and give up your children, your family, and every experience that came with them.

Stay in the relationship, and Fred vanishes again with hurt feelings.

Which do you pick? Would you choose Fred’s feelings over those children? Over that family?

When you reframe it this way, the cost is no longer abstract hurt feelings. It’s the concrete loss of a whole branch of your life—a loss you’d be expected to accept because Fred couldn’t put his emotions aside. (Yes, I’m steering you here. Stay with me.)

Now switch to Fred’s perspective:

Your friend has fallen in love with someone you yourself used to love, and she has fallen in love with him too. That hurts badly, so you tell them you do not approve.

In effect, you are telling Bob (and Olympia):

“Because this hurts my feelings, you should never pursue this woman, never build a life with her, and never have the children you might have had with her.”

Would you accept that bargain for yourself?

Would you look back at 70 or 80 and think, “I’m glad I stayed single / chose someone else / had no children with her so my friend wouldn’t feel bad”?

And would you really want to deny your friend that experience—the life you once wished for?

Let’s step back from the emotions and use a more objective frame: startups.

Say you tried many times to start a startup and failed. Your friend suddenly has a similar idea, just repackaged. Would you tell him he can’t start it because you failed at yours?

Now say he goes ahead anyway and succeeds. Would you guilt-trip him for starting a similar company and making it work?

Imagine he starts it and fails. Would you guilt-trip him for trying the same mission you once tried?

So ultimately, as Bob, which choice is correct? I don’t think a clean “correct” choice or wholly positive outcome exists. It comes down mostly to the realistic chances of something great. If you connect very deeply with Olympia and the odds of building a family are strong, the sanest option is to pursue it. If Fred cannot put his feelings aside for your shot at something immeasurably valuable, do you even want him as a deep relationship in your life?
